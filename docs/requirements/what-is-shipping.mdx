# Shipping is hard

Most people don’t know how to ship a project. Even engineers at big tech companies.

\[comic: \]

People can build incredible things without shipping it. But if it’s only in your room, on your computer… **Does it actually exist?**

## The definition of shipping

<div class="-mt-4">
  <small>_Modified from @alexren’s [hwdocs.hackclub.com](https://hwdocs.hackclub.dev/shipping/)_</small>
</div>

There’s no perfect definition, but **shipping is the process of actually making your project sharable.** It’s _the_ most important part of your project, almost as important as the entire project itself.

When you first make something, it lives as files on your computer. This is bad.

It’s not real and in the world. Only YOU can access it, nobody else can see it. When you look back at the project a few years from now, it’ll be very, very difficult to remember anything about it\!

### Shipping can be boiled down to three concepts. These are the MINIMUM for shipping.

1. Document what your project actually is

2. Understandable and organized

3. Public, and online

\[image: chart\]

## In more concrete terms, here’s how to ship for Fallout:

### 1. Document what your project actually is:

- Why did you build it? Your motivation and story
- A short description of what it does\!
- A brief on how it all fits together Photos\! Of your design, etc.

### 2. Understandable and organized.

Make all files & resources easily accessible & organized. It should be customizable (include source files & step files\!)

**When you make your project nothing but a dump of files and 2 sentences for a README**, it’s hard for other people to recognize your work, to learn from it. _It’s not real_. It only lives on in your tiny corner of this earth.

### 3. Public, and online.

Put it on a public repository on GitHub\!
